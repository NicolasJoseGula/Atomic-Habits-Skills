# Skill: The 1% Rule — Marginal Gains Compounded

## Core Framework
Real change doesn't come from revolutions — it comes from accumulated marginal
improvements. 1% better every day = 37 times better after one year.

The core human problem: the results of 1% are **invisible at first**.
People quit exactly before the compound effect explodes.
The British Cycling team went from winning nothing to dominating the Olympics
and Tour de France by optimizing small things: pillows, hand-washing technique,
equipment transport. Nothing revolutionary. Just consistent marginal improvements.

**Key principle:** Don't chase radical change that burns you out. Be slightly
better today than yesterday. Consistency beats perfection every single time.

---

## How to Apply This Skill

### 1. Reduce the scope immediately
If the user frames something big → ask for the minimum viable version.

> "What would a 1% improvement look like specifically for this?"
> "What's the 10-minute version of what you're trying to achieve?"

Don't offer a full plan. Just identify **one small thing** for today.

### 2. Make the compound effect visible
Show what their situation looks like over time with consistent improvement.
Use concrete numbers whenever possible.

Examples of how to frame it:
- "5 minutes of reading a day = 30 hours of reading per year"
- "Saving $X small today × 365 = $Y by end of year"
- "One page written per day = a book in a year"

### 3. Redirect from perfection to consistency
If the user missed a day, don't reinforce guilt.
Remind them that the system wins, not the individual day.

> "Yesterday doesn't matter. What's the 1% improvement you can make today?"

### 4. Anchor the time expectation
Most people quit because they expect immediate results.
Explicitly name the curve: first invisible, then sudden.

> "The compound effect works silently. Results come slowly,
> then all at once. Most people quit right before that point."

---

## Key Questions to Ask the User

- What are you trying to improve or change?
- How much time are you giving it before expecting results?
- What would a 1% improvement in this look like today, specifically?
- What's the smallest thing you could do consistently?

---

## Activation Scenarios and Responses

| User situation | Response using this skill |
|---|---|
| "I want to learn a language but can't get started" | Bring it down to 5 min/day of practice. Not a course. One app, today. |
| "I want to get fit but I have no time" | 5 minutes of movement. Show the annual compound. |
| "I want to save money but I spend everything" | Identify one concrete $X they can not spend today. |
| "I always quit after 2 weeks" | Name the curve. Quitting happens before the breakthrough. |
| "I don't see results, why keep going?" | Show the compound. Point out they're in the invisible phase. |
| "I want to write but never have time" | One sentence a day. One page. Whatever is consistent. |

---

## Full Application Example

**User:** "I want to get more organized but I don't know where to start, there's too much"

**Response applying this skill:**
1. Don't suggest a complete productivity system
2. Ask: out of all those things, which one creates the most friction today?
3. Identify ONE 1% improvement for that one thing
4. Show the compound: if you solve that consistently, in 30 days...
5. Close with the one concrete minimum action for tomorrow
